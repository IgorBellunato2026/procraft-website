Bathrooms, In Belgravia

Bathrooms designed for Regency
terraces and the mews behind them.

Belgravia is unusual even by Prime Central London standards. Almost the entire neighbourhood — the white-stucco Regency terraces around Belgrave Square, Eaton Square, Chester Square, Lyall Street and Wilton Crescent — is listed and held on long leases from the Grosvenor Estate. Refurbishing a bathroom here means working inside two consent regimes simultaneously: planning and listed-building consent through the City of Westminster, and freeholder consent through Grosvenor.

The bathroom brief reflects the building. Belgravia townhouses are typically four to five storeys, with bathrooms positioned on the upper floors above formal reception rooms. The technical demand on tanking and acoustic specification is high. The aesthetic demand is equally high: the finished room has to read as if it has always belonged in the building.

We deliver both halves. The visible work is finished to hotel standard at residential scale; the underlying construction is built to the technical specification the building actually requires.

Belgravia Considerations

Where Belgravia bathrooms
most often go wrong.

The bathrooms that fail in Belgravia fail in predictable ways. Tanking cut short to keep a programme on track, leading to a leak through three storeys of formal reception room. Listed-building consent overlooked because the work was internal. Grosvenor freeholder consent forgotten until the client gets the letter. Stone laid without proper substrate prep so it cracks in the first heating season.

None of these are inevitable. Each is a function of cutting the wrong corner, by a contractor who does not understand the address. We have refurbished bathrooms across SW1W and SW1X long enough to know which corners can be cut and which cannot — and we never cut the latter.
