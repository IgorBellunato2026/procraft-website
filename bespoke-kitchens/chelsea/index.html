Kitchens, In Chelsea

A kitchen for a Chelsea house
is a different brief.

The housing stock between Sloane Square and World’s End is unusually consistent: Victorian and Georgian terraces, intimate mews properties, and the mansion blocks that line Cheyne Walk and the Embankment. None of them were designed around a contemporary kitchen, and none of them respond well to a kitchen designed for somewhere else.

The brief in Chelsea is almost always the same in shape, even if the materials differ. Open up the rear of the ground floor where it makes sense. Insert a bespoke kitchen that reads as if the architect drew it in 1885. Restore the original detail around it — cornicing, panelling, sash windows — rather than stripping it out. Get the proportions right, and the rest follows.

This is the work we do. We design and build kitchens specifically for Chelsea’s housing stock, in materials and detailing that belong in it. The cabinetry is hand-built in our workshop. The worktops are templated on site, slab-matched at every joint. The installation is done by the carpenters who built the cabinetry, not a sub-contracted team.

Chelsea Considerations

The detail that separates a kitchen
that belongs from one that doesn’t.

A bespoke kitchen in a Chelsea Victorian terrace is not the same brief as a bespoke kitchen in a new-build apartment. The variables that matter are different, the constraints are different, and the assumptions that work elsewhere fail here.

We have refurbished kitchens across Chelsea for long enough to know what those variables are, where the planning friction lives, and how to design around the constraints rather than fight them. Original ceiling heights, narrow stair access, mews delivery windows, party-wall sensitivities, freeholder rules in mansion blocks — all of it is factored in at quote stage, not discovered mid-project.
