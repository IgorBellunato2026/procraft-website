Refurbishment, In Belgravia

A full refurbishment in Belgravia
is a layered consent project.

Belgravia’s housing stock is unusually consistent and almost entirely listed: Cubitt-built white-stucco Regency terraces around Belgrave Square, Eaton Square and Chester Square, with mews houses tucked behind, all under Grosvenor Estate freehold. None of them respond well to a generic main-contractor approach.

The brief in Belgravia is rarely cosmetic. Most of our full refurbishments here involve listed-building consent, Grosvenor Estate approvals, internal structural alterations, full re-services and the restoration of original detail in parallel with new bespoke joinery, kitchens and bathrooms. The programme has to be planned around all of it from day one — not improvised when the wall comes down.

This is the work we do. We design and run the project end-to-end. The same crew that takes the kitchen apart builds the new one. The same carpenters that strip the panelling restore it. There is one point of contact for the entire programme, one written specification, one schedule, and one final walk-through.

Belgravia Considerations

The variables that decide
whether a refurbishment runs.

A full house refurbishment in a Belgravia listed Regency terrace is not the same brief as a refurbishment in a new-build apartment. The constraints are different, the consent environment is more layered, and the standard the building holds you to is different.

We have refurbished Belgravia homes long enough to know how the Belgravia Conservation Area rules apply in practice, where listed-building consent is non-negotiable, how Grosvenor Estate freeholder approvals work, and how to deliver into mews addresses where access matters as much as the cabinetry. All of it is factored in at quote stage and built into the programme.
